Techniques and Protocols in Dental CT:

Dental CT utilizes different field-of-view (FOV) sizes depending on the specific clinical scenario:

Small FOV CBCT

Ideal for localized imaging, focusing on a small region, such as a single tooth or implant site.

Medium FOV CBCT

Used for broader imaging covering multiple teeth or regions, such as the full arch.

Bone Quality
Lekholm and Zarb proposed a method to categoerised bone quality in the Dental CTs.
  1. Quality 1: Homogeneous compact bone (HU > 1250)
  2. Quality 2: Thick compact bone with dense trabecular core (HU: 850-1250)
  3. Quality 3: Thin cortical bone around dense trabecular (HU 350-850)
  4. Quality 4: Low-density trabecular bone (HU 150-350)
Important Anatomical Landmarks

Following anatomical landmarks must be carefully reviewed in all cases:

Inferior Alveolar Canal / Mandibular nerve canal

Starts posteriorly at the mandibular foramen on the lingual plate. Runs anteriorly, forms a small loop before exiting at the mental foramen apical to the 2nd premolar or between the bicuspids.

Retromolar canal and its variations
  1. The retromolar triangle (RMT) is a depressed area formed by the bifurcation of the mandibular temporal crest and the posterior edge of the last mandibular molar.
  2. The gross anatomy of the RMT, which is an area of great importance in dental practice, varies greatly according to the presence of the mandibular third molar and alveolar bone resorption.
  3. The retromolar canal (RMC) normally arises from the mandibular canal behind the third molar, and travels anterosuperiorly to the retromolar foramen (RMF), which is located within or around the RMT.
  4. Several studies on the RMC and RMF have reported considerable diversity with a frequency from 1.7% to 72% depending on the study design and the race of the participants.
  5. Knowledge of the location of the RMC and of its anatomical variations can facilitate surgical intervention and protect the patient from the potential complications thereof.
  6. The RMT is relevant to surgical procedures, such as extraction of an impacted third molar, dental implant treatment, bone harvesting as a donor site for bone graft surgery, and sagittal split ramus osteotomy. These surgicalb proecdures poses a risk o iatrogenic injuries to the contetns of RMC.
  7. The neurovascular content of the RMC contains predominantly thin nerve fascicles branching from the inferior alveolar nerve, small arteries, and numerous venules accompanying those arteries. Therefore, damage to the RMC contents could lead to un- expected bleeding and iatrogenic nerve damage, potentially resulting in paresthesia.
  8. In clinical practice, anatomical variations, such as supplemental or accessory canals and foramina, can only be detected by radiologic methods. However, conventional two-dimensional (2D) radiographs such as panoramic im- ages are insufficient for this purpose- sectional imaging, such cone-beam CT (CBCT), can successfully demonstrate RMC and its vraitions.

Lingual Canal.
  1. Lingual canla contains Sublingual and submental arteries.There is tremendous varations in their course and anastosmosis.
  2. Performing the dental implantation procedures in the anterior and posterior regions of the mandible can result in perforation of the lingual cortex, which could injure the arteries that lie within the lingual canal. If hemorrhage occurs, it could lead to life-threatening upper airway obstruction.
  3. Pre-surgical investigation to identify the diameter of the lingual foramina and canal as well as their distance from the alveolar process might be necessary to prevent hemorrhage for all patients.
Arteries in dental CT

The major detnal arteries arise from Maxillary artery

Important arteries from cranial to caudal are as follows:

  • Descending palatine artery-:

    The Descending Palatine Artery descends through the pterygopalatine canal , emerging from the greater palatine foramen, runs forward in a groove on the medial side of the alveolar border of the hard palate to the incisive canal

    The terminal branch of the artery passes upward through this canal to anastomose with the sphenopalatine artery. Branches are distributed to the gums, the palatine glands, and the mucous membrane of the roof of the mouth; while in the pterygopalatine canal it gives off twigs which descend in the lesser palatine canals to supply the soft palate and palatine tonsil, anastomosing with the ascending palatine artery.

    It terminates into two branches :

    1. Inferiorly into (posteriorly directed) lesser palatine artery
    2. And (anteriorly directed) greater palatine artery. Greater palatine nerve runs along with Descending Palatine artery
