Mandibular Nerve

Introduction

Mandibular (V3) nerve is the largest of the three divisions of the Fifth cranial nerve (Trigeminal Nerve).

Innervation

Mandibular nerve innervates mainly

  1. Mandible and lower teeth
  2. Oral mucosa and anterior 1/3 of tongue and lower lip
  3. TMJ
  4. Muscle of mastication
  5. Sensory to skin of temporal region, auricle, and external acoustic meatus

1. Origin and Roots

Brainstem Nuclei: Mesencephalic nucleus, Principal sensory nucleus, Spinal nucleus form the large sensory root. Motor nucleus (pons tegmentum) forms the motor root.

2. Nerve Path through Skull

Motor root joins sensory root in the trigeminal cave. Both roots exit the skull via foramen ovale and enter the infratemporal fossa.

3. Branches in Infratemporal Fossa

Sensory Branch: Meningeal branch
Motor Branches:

  • Medial pterygoid muscle
  • Tensor tympani muscle
  • Tensor veli palatini muscle

4. Main Trunk Division

Smaller Anterior Trunk (Motor and Sensory)

  • Temporalis, Masseter, and Lateral pterygoid muscles
  • Buccal nerve
Larger Posterior Trunk (Mainly Sensory)
  • Auriculotemporal nerve: around medial meningeal artery
  • Lingual Nerve: to anterior two-thirds of tongue
  • Inferior Alveolar Nerve:
    • Mylohyoid and anterior belly of digastric
    • Mandibular teeth, chin, and lower lip

Clinico-Radiological Significance

Below is of points relevant in clinical radiology regarding the Mandibular nerve

Surgical Considerations
  • Mandibular Nerve Role: Is crucial in oral and maxillofacial surgery, especially in complex procedures involving nerve block anesthesia.
  • Anatomic Complexity: The infratemporal fossais known for its complex, confined anatomy. Surgeons face challenges due to the proximity of critical structures such as the internal maxillary artery and pterygoid plexus.
  • Inferior Alveolar Nerve Variations: Variations of the inferior alveolar nerve can impact anesthesia effectiveness. These variations are known to mimic keratocystic odontogenic tumors in imaging, posing diagnostic challenges.
Clinical Significance
  • Challenges in Mandibular Nerve Anesthesia: Achieving complete mandibular nerve block is difficult due to nerve variations in the foramen ovale region. SOM and Curtin describes how anatomical variations in the nerve branches can lead to incomplete anesthesia, impacting dental and surgical outcomes.
  • Temporomandibular Joint Dysfunction (TMD):
    • TMD involves pain and dysfunction of the jaw muscles and joints, often linked to mandibular nerve branches that control jaw movements.
    • The auriculotemporal nerve is particularly vulnerable during TMJ surgeries, and damage can cause sensory disturbances in the ear and auricle regions.
  • Trigeminal Neuralgia:
    • Classical Type: Often due to vascular compression at the root entry zone of the trigeminal nerve, leading to intense facial pain.
    • Secondary Type: Occurs with conditions like multiple sclerosis, tumors, or inflammation affecting the mandibular nerve branches, as discussed in SOM and Curtin.
    • Treatment: Surgical intervention by severing the sensory root is effective for some patients, providing relief from chronic pain.
  • Radiation Ear Pain and Auriculotemporal Nerve: Radiation pain along the auriculotemporal nerve has been documented, especially in patients with malignancies like lingual cancer. This pain is often misdiagnosed due to overlapping nerve pathways.
  • Tonic Tensor Tympani Syndrome (TTTS):
    • Characterized by ear pain, fluttering sensations, or a full feeling in the ear, TTTS is often seen in patients with hyperacusis or heightened sensitivity to sound.
    • Increased activity in the tensor tympani muscle can exacerbate symptoms, often triggered by anxiety or stress.
